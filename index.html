Systemic Pathology

Will discuss these general pathological processes within specific organ systems. Each lecture will focus on a particular system, explaining how processes like necrosis, hemorrhage, ischemia, and inflammation manifest within that specialized system.

Emphasis on Inflammation

Key Concept: A key concept that will be frequently encountered and emphasized throughout the systemic pathology lectures is inflammation. It is crucial to have a good understanding of inflammation, as it forms the basis for understanding many systemic pathologies.

Vascular Changes in Inflammation

Hyperemia

Definition: Hyperemia means an increase in the amount of blood inside the blood vessels.

Additional notes: When there is inflammation, there might also be hemorrhage and increased vascular permeability.

Gross and Microscopic Appearance of Inflammation

Gross Appearance

  • Redness: Due to hyperemia, the tissue will appear red
  • Swelling: This occurs due to edema
  • Hemorrhage: May also be present
  • Degeneration and Necrosis: The affected tissue will show degeneration and necrosis

Microscopic Appearance

  • Degeneration and necrosis will also be visible microscopically

Types of Acute Inflammation

Catarrhal

Features: Mucous exudate on mucosa, hyperplasia of goblet cells

Common in: Digestive system (mucosa lining)

Serous

Common in: Serous membranes (pericardium, pleura, peritoneum)

Suppurative

Microscopic: Presence of neutrophils

Cause: Pyogenic microorganisms

Fibrinous

Gross: Pseudomembrane on mucosa surface

Microscopic: Fibrin network

Hemorrhagic

Gross: Obvious hemorrhage

Microscopic: Large number of RBCs with inflammatory cells

Eosinophilic

Associated with: Hypersensitivity reactions

Stomatitis: Inflammation of the Oral Mucosa

Definition: Stomatitis is the inflammation of the oral mucosa. The term "stoma" in Latin means oral cavity.

Causes of Stomatitis

Physical Trauma

  • Thorns, wood, or glass pieces
  • Irregular, sharp teeth or feeding utensils
  • Hot drenches or frozen foods
  • Electric burns

Chemical Agents

  • Alkaline or corrosive acids

Nutritional Deficiencies

  • Hypovitaminosis A
  • Niacin deficiency

Microorganisms

Bacteria
  • Actinomyces bovis
  • Actinobacillus lignieresi
  • Fusobacterium necrophorum
  • Corynebacterium pyogenes
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Streptococci and Staphylococci
Fungi
  • Monilia albicans (also known as Candida albicans)
Viruses
Vesicular Stomatitis
  • Foot and Mouth Disease (FMD)
  • Vesicular Exanthema
  • Vesicular Stomatitis
Erosive and Ulcerative Stomatitis
  • Cattle Plague (Rinderpest)
  • Bovine Viral Diarrhea
  • Malignant Catarrhal Fever
  • Blue Tongue, Sheep Pox

Detailed Types of Stomatitis

Catarrhal Stomatitis

Considered the simplest type of inflammation in the oral cavity.

Gross Lesions

  • Hyperemic mucosa: Red and swollen
  • Edematous appearance: Dull gray surface
  • Excessive mucous production
  • Rapid degeneration and desquamation

Microscopic Features

  • Hyperplasia of goblet cells
  • Hyperplasia of lymphoid tissues
  • Inflammatory cell infiltration

Suppurative Stomatitis

Causes: Result of trauma and pyogenic microorganism complications

Lesions: Characterized by the presence of pus, usually removed by food movement

Fibrinous Stomatitis

Characterized by: Deposition of fibrin threads on the mucosa

Vesicular Stomatitis

Definition: Stomatitis characterized by the formation of vesicles. It occurs in most domestic animal species.

Specific Viral Causes

Vesicular Stomatitis Virus

Can affect horses, cattle, and pigs. Does not cause cardiac lesions.

FMD (Foot and Mouth Disease)

Causes interstitial myocarditis in young calves, which can be fatal.

Swine Vesicular Disease

Mainly affects pigs.

Pathogenesis

1

Ballooning Degeneration

Process begins with ballooning degeneration (hydroptic degeneration) in epithelial cells of the stratum spinosum. Cells fill with water.

2

Vesicle Formation

Water-filled cells rupture and several ruptured cells coalesce to form cavities containing vesicles

3

Bullae Formation

Multiple vesicles can coalesce to form larger ones called bullae

4

Erosion

Vesicles rupture due to tongue movement, leaving behind erosions with intact basement membrane

Erosive and Ulcerative Stomatitis

Definition: Stomatitis characterized by local epithelial defects of the oral mucosa

Causes

  • Cattle Plague (Rinderpest)
  • Bovine Viral Diarrhea
  • Malignant Catarrhal Fever

Erosions

  • Loss of superficial epithelium layer
  • Intact basement membrane
  • Vary in size and shape
  • Heal by regeneration without scarring

Ulcers

  • Extensive loss including basement membrane
  • Expose underlying lamina propria
  • Elevated and ragged edges
  • Heal by scar formation

Necrotic Stomatitis

Definition: Stomatitis associated with severe necrosis

Causes

Physical/Chemical
  • Thermal agents
  • Chemical agents
Bacterial
  • Fusobacterium necrophorum
  • Other anaerobes
  • Causes Calf Diphtheria

Gross Lesions

  • Yellow-gray, round necrotic foci
  • Distinct rim of hyperemic tissue
  • Acute necrotizing ulcerative inflammation

Microscopic Features

  • Well-demarcated coagulation necrosis
  • Associated inflammatory reaction
  • Hyperemia around necrotic areas

Diseases Affecting the Tongue

Actinobacillosis (Wooden Tongue)

Definition: An important infection of the tongue, giving it a woody consistency

Causative Agent: Actinobacillus lignieresi

Species Affected: Principally cattle, occasionally horses and small ruminants

Pathogenesis

Actinobacillus lignieresi is an opportunistic invader of damaged lingual tissue. It causes a characteristic pyogranulomatous inflammatory reaction.

Understanding Granuloma

Granuloma: A type of chronic productive inflammation containing specific cell types:

  • Epithelioid cells (modified macrophages)
  • Lymphocytes
  • Plasma cells
  • Multinucleated Langhans giant cells

Pyogranuloma: A granuloma that also contains neutrophils

Lesions

Gross Appearance
  • Tongue becomes hard and woody
  • Characteristic woody consistency
Microscopic Features
Central Core

Contains colonies of Actinobacilli

Middle Layer

Radiating amorphous, eosinophilic, club-like material ("eosinophilic clubs" or "Indian clubs") - result of antigen-antibody reactions

Outer Layer

Granulomatous cells: neutrophils, lymphocytes, epithelioid cells, and multinucleated Langhans giant cells

Salivary Glands

Ptyalism

Definition: Abnormal or excessive accumulation and discharge of saliva in the mouth (increased secretion)

Causes:

  • Heavy metal poisoning
  • Organophosphate poisoning
  • Encephalitis
  • Stomatitis (oral inflammation)

Special Note: In Foot and Mouth Disease (FMD), characteristic "ropy salivation" is observed, where strings of saliva hang from the mouth.

Aptyalism

Definition: Reduced or ceased secretion of saliva

Causes:

  • Fever
  • Dehydration
  • Salivary gland disease (e.g., duct obstruction)

Sialadenitis

Definition: Inflammation of the salivary glands

Sialolithiasis

Definition: The formation of concentrations or "stones" in the salivary duct or in the gland itself

Note: The term "lithiasis" generally refers to stones (e.g., "urolithiasis" for urinary stones)

More common in horses than other species

Ranula

Definition: Cystic distension of the salivary duct in the floor of the mouth

Contents: May be serous or thick tenacious mucus

Formation: Occurs when there is an obstruction in the duct, leading to fluid accumulation

Esophagus

Choke

Definition: A complete or partial obstruction of the esophagus by foreign material

Causes by Species

Cattle

Beets, turnips, or small ears of corn

Horses

Gradual accumulation of ground or whole grain

Dogs and Cats

Sharp pieces of bone

Lesions

Dry Tympany (Bloat)

In ruminants: Occurs because obstruction prevents regurgitation and eructation of gases

Esophageal Diverticulum

In horses: Sac-like dilatation of the esophageal wall

Perforation

Due to sharp foreign objects (e.g., bone ends)

Necrosis and Gangrene

At the point of obstruction

Congestion and Hemorrhage

In perforated cases

Esophageal Parasites

Common Examples

  • Gongylonema spp. (ruminants, pigs, horses)
  • Gasterophilus spp. (horses)
  • Hypoderma lineatum (ruminants)

Spirocerca lupi (Dogs)

Most pathogenic esophageal parasite

Migration Pattern

These nematodes migrate from the stomach to reach the esophageal submucosa by passing through the aorta.

Lesions
Reactive Granulomas

Nodules formed in the esophageal wall containing adult worms. The body treats the large parasite as a foreign body, forming a granuloma around it.

Aneurysm of Thoracic Aorta

Abnormal widening or dilation in the aortic wall where the parasite passed.

Tumors (Rare)

Rarely, esophageal fibrosarcomas or osteosarcomas may develop.

Esophagitis

Definition: Inflammation of the esophagus

Causes

Trauma

Due to foreign bodies

Chemicals

Acids, alkaloids

Infection

Bovine Viral Diarrhea, Spirocerca lupi

Nutritional

Vitamin A deficiency

Commonly affects: Young animals

Pathology of Forestomachs (Rumen, Reticulum, Omasum)

The key conditions affecting the forestomachs include:

Tympany or Bloat Ruminal Atony and Impaction Ruminitis, Reticulitis, and Omasitis Traumatic Reticuloperitonitis

Ruminal Tympany (Bloat)

Definition: Accumulation of excessive quantities of gas in the rumen and reticulum. It can be acute or chronic (recurrent).

Classification of Ruminal Tympany

Frothy Tympany (Primary Tympany)

Definition: Occurs when fermentation gases are trapped in very small bubbles, forming a stable foam that cannot be easily eructated. The coalescence of gas bubbles is inhibited.

Causes
Sudden Changes from Dry to Green Ration

Examples: Alfalfa or clover (known as pasture bloat)

Pathogenesis:
  • Legumes are rapidly digested in the rumen, leading to high concentration of fine particles that trap gas bubbles
  • Soluble proteins from these plants act as foaming agents
  • Nonvolatile acids from legumes and ruminal fermentation lower rumen pH to 5-6, optimal for foam formation
Feeding Large Amounts of Cereal Grains
  • Rapid digestion and abundance of small particles trap gas in bubbles
  • Some bacteria species (abundant in high concentrate rations) produce insoluble slime that promotes stable foam formation

Dry Tympany (Secondary Tympany)

Definition: Occurs when the animal cannot eructate free gas, even though gas production itself might be normal.

Causes
Obstruction of the Esophagus

Example: Choke

Muscular Weakness of the Rumen

Due to toxic production affecting muscles or nerves responsible for rumen motility

Pathognomonic Lesion of Tympany

Bloat Line

Most characteristic and diagnostic lesion of tympany

A sharp line of demarcation in the esophageal mucosa that divides the esophagus into two distinct areas:

Above the Line (Cranial)

In the neck region, towards the head: Shows congestion and hemorrhage due to pressure from the distended rumen

Below the Line (Caudal)

Distal to the thoracic inlet: Appears pale and bloodless (ischemic)

Important Note: Carcass distension with gas is not a reliable diagnostic lesion because fermentation can occur after death, leading to gas production even in non-bloated animals.

Other Lesions of Tympany

  • Congestion and hemorrhages in the cranial thorax, neck, and head
  • The lungs are atelectatic (collapsed alveoli) due to pressure from the distended rumen
  • Pressure ischemia of the abdominal viscera, especially the liver

Ruminal Atony and Impaction

Definition: Overdistension of the rumen with indigestible food, with little or no gas production.

Causes

  • Overfeeding with large amounts of highly fermentable carbohydrate feeds
  • Penetration of the rumen or reticulum wall by sharp foreign bodies
  • Lack of water
  • Defective mastication and salivation due to teeth defects or tongue lesions
  • Injury of the vagus nerve (causing vagus indigestion)

Pathogenesis (in Overfeeding Carbohydrates)

1

After ingesting large amounts of carbohydrate-rich feeds

2

Fermentation process rapidly produces lactic acid

3

This results in lowering of the pH of ruminal contents

4

Consequently, rumen motility decreases and may lead to complete stasis

Pathognomonic Lesions

  • The rumen is overdistended with indigestible food
  • The wall of the rumen is thin; the cornified epithelium is soft and peels off easily
  • The ruminal contents are dull in color, solid, and have a bad putrefying odor
  • Petechial hemorrhages may be found in serous membranes

Inflammation of Forestomachs

Ruminitis

Definition: Inflammation of the rumen in ruminant animals, characterized by seropurulent exudate and ulcer formation.

Causes:

  • Change in diet (e.g., corn or alfalfa hay)
  • Chemicals/drugs
  • Fusobacterium necrophorum infection (especially with necrosis)

Reticulitis

Definition: Inflammation of the reticulum in ruminant animals, characterized by abscess formation, adhesions, peritonitis, and pericarditis.

Causes:

  • Trauma or perforation by foreign body
  • Sharp objects like needles and wires

Omasitis

Definition: Inflammation of the omasum in ruminant animals.

Causes:

  • Actinobacillus sp. (specifically Actinobacillus lignieresi)
  • Leads to granulomatous inflammatory reaction

Additional condition: Impaction of the omasum can also occur due to very dry feed or lack of water, leading to loss of motility.

Traumatic Reticuloperitonitis (Hardware Disease)

Definition: Perforation of the forestomachs by foreign bodies. It is typically caused by a sharp foreign body, usually a wire or nail longer than 4 cm, which penetrates the reticular wall.

Patterns of Perforation

Incomplete Perforation

The foreign body enters the wall of the reticulum but does not pass through to another structure.

Result: Usually causes suppurative or granulomatous inflammation within the reticular wall. Generally considered inconsequential compared to complete perforation.

Complete Perforation

The foreign body fully penetrates the reticular wall and exits into the surrounding tissues or organs.

Patterns of Complete Perforation
Cranioventral Penetration (Forward and Downward)
Short or Bent Foreign Body

May only reach the peritoneum, causing acute local peritonitis. If withdrawn later, can lead to chronic local peritonitis with adhesions.

Longer Foreign Body

May perforate the diaphragm and then the pericardium, resulting in traumatic pericarditis (fibrinous or serofibrinous inflammation of the pericardium).

Ventral Penetration (Downward)

May result in a subperitoneal abscess (under the peritoneum) or a subcutaneous abscess (under the skin).

Right Lateral Deviation (To the Right Side)

The penetrating agent involves the wall of the abomasum.

Sequelae of Traumatic Reticuloperitonitis

These are the consequences or complications that can arise from the perforation:

Pericarditis

If the pericardium is affected, it can lead to serofibrinous or purulent pericarditis, possibly with hypertrophied myocardium.

Vagus Indigestion

If the ventral branch of the vagus nerve is affected, it results in digestive disturbances.

Sudden Death from Hemorrhage

Can occur if one of the larger regional arteries is perforated.

Sudden Death from Heart Damage

May occur if there is penetration of the myocardium (heart muscle) or rupture of a coronary artery.

Pneumonia and Pleuritis

Penetration into the thoracic cavity (without pericardial perforation) can lead to inflammation of the lungs and pleura.

Diaphragmatic Hernia

May occur due to weakening of the diaphragm by lesions produced by the foreign body.

Metastatic Abscesses in the Liver

Bacteria from the site of infection can travel via the bloodstream (metastatic spread) to the liver, forming abscesses there.

Gastritis and Abomasitis

Gastritis

Inflammation of the stomach

Abomasitis

Inflammation of the abomasum (the true stomach in ruminants)

Types of Gastritis

Acute Gastritis

  • Catarrhal Gastritis
  • Hemorrhagic Gastritis

Chronic Gastritis

  • Hypertrophic Gastritis
  • Granulomatous Gastritis

Acute Catarrhal Gastritis

Causes

  • Physical agents (cold or hot)
  • Chemical agents
  • Parasitic gastritis
  • Canine distemper and feline enteritis
  • Nutritional deficiency

Gross Lesions

  • Increased reddening and thickening of the mucosa
  • Thick mucous exudate covering the mucosa

Histopathology (Microscopic)

  • Hyperemia and desquamation of epithelium
  • Leukocytic infiltrations in mucosa and submucosa
  • Lymphoid hyperplasia of gastric mucous membrane lymphocytic foci (in processes of some days' duration)

Acute Hemorrhagic Gastritis

Causes

Acute Infectious Diseases

  • Clostridium septicum (causing Braxy in sheep)
  • Cattle Plague
  • Leptospirosis

Systemic Toxicity

  • Uremia
  • Arsenic poisoning

Note: Hemorrhagic inflammation is often caused by severe toxins from bacteria or systemic toxicities.

Gross Lesions

  • The mucosa is bright-red in color
  • Free blood is found on the surface or in the gastric contents
  • The blood appears black due to the formation of acid hematin

Histopathology (Microscopic)

  • The predominant exudate is erythrocytes (red blood cells)
  • Dilatation of the gastric blood vessels
  • Leukocytic cell infiltration

Chronic Gastritis

Hypertrophic Gastritis

Causes

  • Habronema spp. (common in horses)
  • Trichostrongylus axei (common in horses)

Gross Lesions

  • Thickened rugae (folds of the mucosa)

Note: These folds might look like "rogi"

Microscopic Features

  • Hyperplasia of gastric glands (increased cell number)
  • Metaplasia of lining epithelium into goblet cells (cell type change)
  • Infiltration of mucosa with chronic inflammatory cells and fibroblasts
  • Hypertrophy of smooth muscle fibers (increase in cell size)

Granulomatous Gastritis

Definition: Inflammation associated with the formation of granulomas in the stomach.

Causes

  • Draschia megastoma (a parasite)

Gross Lesions

  • Appear as raised nodules
  • Fine, thread-like adult worms may be found in the center of the nodule

Microscopic Features

  • A classic granuloma occupying the submucosa
  • Center contains cross-section of the parasite
  • Surrounded by concentric layers of fibrous connective tissue and inflammatory cells

Granuloma contains typical cells:

  • Epithelioid cells
  • Lymphocytes
  • Giant cells (like Langhans cells)
  • Possibly neutrophils
