Emphasis on Inflammation

A key concept that will be frequently encountered and emphasized throughout the systemic pathology lectures is inflammation. It is crucial to have a good understanding of inflammation, as it forms the basis for understanding many systemic pathologies.

Vascular Changes During Inflammation

  • Hyperemia: Primary vascular change - increase in the amount of blood inside the blood vessels
  • Hemorrhage: May also occur during inflammation
  • Increased vascular permeability: Additional change that occurs

Acute inflammation is typically accompanied by hyperemia and hemorrhage.

Gross Appearance

  • Redness: Due to hyperemia
  • Swelling: Due to edema
  • Hemorrhage: May be present
  • Degeneration and Necrosis: Affected tissue shows these changes

Microscopic Appearance

  • Degeneration and necrosis visible microscopically

In the oral cavity, we commonly encounter:

  • Catarrhal inflammation
  • Suppurative inflammation
  • Fibrinous inflammation
  • Hemorrhagic inflammation
  • Serous inflammation (more in serous membranes)

Pathology of the Oral Cavity

Stomatitis: Inflammation of the Oral Mucosa

Definition: Stomatitis is the inflammation of the oral mucosa. The term "stoma" in Latin means oral cavity.

Causes of Stomatitis

  1. Physical Trauma:
    • Thorns, wood, or glass pieces
    • Coarse feed or hay
    • Dental problems (sharp teeth, broken teeth)
  2. Chemical Agents:
    • Caustic substances
    • Acids or alkalis
  3. Infectious Agents:
    • Bacteria: Actinobacillus lignieresii, Fusobacterium necrophorum
    • Fungi: Monilia albicans (Candida albicans)
    • Viruses: Vesicular Stomatitis, Foot and Mouth Disease, etc.

Classification of Stomatitis

According to Location
  • Diffuse Stomatitis: Affects entire oral cavity
  • Focal Stomatitis: Affects a specific area of the oral cavity
According to Severity and Morphology
  • Catarrhal stomatitis
  • Suppurative stomatitis
  • Fibrinous stomatitis
  • Vesicular stomatitis
  • Erosive and ulcerative stomatitis
  • Necrotic stomatitis

Detailed Types of Stomatitis

Catarrhal Stomatitis

Considered the simplest type of inflammation in the oral cavity.

Causes: Trauma, chemical irritants, and infectious agents.

Lesions:

  • Grossly: Mucosa is hyperemic (red) and edematous (swollen). Surface undergoes rapid degeneration and desquamation, becoming dull gray. There is excessive mucous production.
  • Microscopically: Hyperplasia of goblet cells and hyperplasia of lymphoid tissues.
Suppurative Stomatitis

Causes: Result of trauma and pyogenic microorganism complications.

Lesions: Characterized by the presence of pus. The pus is usually removed by the movement of food.

Fibrinous Stomatitis

Characterized by: Deposition of fibrin threads on the mucosa.

Vesicular Stomatitis

Definition: Stomatitis characterized by the formation of vesicles. It occurs in most domestic animal species.

Causes: Viral infections (Foot and Mouth Disease, Vesicular Exanthema, Vesicular Stomatitis) and irritant chemicals.

Lesions:

  • Process begins with ballooning degeneration (hydroptic degeneration) in epithelial cells
  • Water-filled cells rupture, forming vesicles
  • Multiple vesicles can coalesce to form bullae
  • Vesicles rupture, leaving behind erosions
Erosive and Ulcerative Stomatitis

Definition: Stomatitis characterized by local epithelial defects of the oral mucosa.

Causes: Primarily viral infections (Cattle Plague, Bovine Viral Diarrhea, Malignant Catarrhal Fever).

Lesions:

  • Erosions: Loss of superficial layer of epithelium, basement membrane intact
  • Ulcers: Extensive loss of epithelium including basement membrane
Necrotic Stomatitis

Definition: Stomatitis associated with severe necrosis.

Causes: Thermal or chemical agents and bacteria like Fusobacterium necrophorum.

Lesions:

  • Grossly: Acute necrotizing ulcerative inflammation with yellow-gray necrotic foci
  • Microscopically: Well-demarcated foci of coagulation necrosis with inflammatory reaction

Glossitis: Inflammation of the Tongue

Glossitis can be considered a form of stomatitis that specifically affects the tongue. It can occur as a primary condition or secondary to other diseases.

Causes of Glossitis

Physical Trauma

Sharp teeth, foreign bodies, or mechanical injury

Chemical Agents

Caustic substances or irritant chemicals

Infectious Agents

Bacteria, fungi, or viruses

Nutritional Deficiencies

Hypovitaminosis, especially B-complex vitamins

Systemic Diseases

Secondary to conditions like Foot and Mouth Disease

Types of Glossitis

Acute Glossitis

  • Sudden onset
  • Severe inflammation
  • Swelling of the tongue
  • Pain and difficulty swallowing

Chronic Glossitis

  • Gradual development
  • Persistent inflammation
  • Atrophy or hypertrophy of tongue papillae
  • May lead to structural changes

Specific Conditions

Wooden Tongue (Actinobacillus lignieresii)

Etiology: Caused by Actinobacillus lignieresii, a gram-negative rod found in the oral cavity of ruminants.

Pathogenesis:

  • Organism penetrates through small wounds in the tongue
  • Forms characteristic sulfur granules
  • Causes chronic inflammation and fibrosis

Lesions:

  • Grossly: Tongue becomes firm and wooden-like
  • Loss of flexibility and mobility
  • Difficulty in prehension and mastication
Focal Glossitis

Causes: Usually due to physical trauma from sharp foreign bodies.

Lesions:

  • Localized inflammation at the site of injury
  • Formation of nodules or ulcers
  • Potential for abscess formation

Salivary Gland Disorders

Salivary glands produce saliva which plays important roles in lubrication, digestion, and protection of the oral cavity. Disorders of these glands can significantly impact an animal's ability to eat and maintain oral health.

Types of Salivary Gland Disorders

Sialadenitis

Definition: Inflammation of the salivary glands.

Causes:

  • Bacterial infections (Actinomyces bovis, Actinobacillus lignieresii)
  • Viral infections
  • Physical trauma
  • Foreign bodies

Lesions:

  • Swelling of the affected gland
  • Pain and discomfort
  • Reduced saliva production
  • Potential abscess formation

Sialadenosis

Definition: Non-inflammatory enlargement of salivary glands.

Causes:

  • Nutritional imbalances
  • Metabolic disorders
  • Toxic substances

Lesions:

  • Diffuse enlargement of glands
  • Normal histological structure maintained
  • Increased saliva production initially, then decreased

Specific Conditions

Actinobacillosis of Salivary Glands

Etiology: Caused by Actinobacillus lignieresii, same organism responsible for wooden tongue.

Pathogenesis:

  • Infection typically occurs through wounds in the oral cavity
  • Organism spreads to regional lymph nodes and salivary glands
  • Formation of sulfur granules and chronic inflammation

Lesions:

  • Firm swelling of mandibular salivary glands
  • Draining sinuses may form
  • Chronic suppurative inflammation
Sialocele (Salivary Mucocele)

Definition: Accumulation of saliva in the subcutaneous tissues due to leakage from a damaged salivary duct or gland.

Causes:

  • Trauma to salivary ducts
  • Neoplasia of salivary glands
  • Idiopathic causes

Lesions:

  • Soft, fluctuant swelling in the neck or under the tongue
  • May become infected
  • Can cause difficulty swallowing or breathing if large

Detailed Esophagus Pathology

The esophagus is a muscular tube that connects the pharynx to the stomach. It serves as a conduit for food and water. Pathological conditions of the esophagus can significantly impact an animal's ability to swallow and maintain proper nutrition.

Common Esophageal Disorders

Choke (Esophageal Obstruction)

Definition: Obstruction of the esophagus by a foreign body.

Causes:

  • Ingestion of large pieces of food without proper mastication
  • Dry feed or poorly prepared feed
  • Foreign bodies like potatoes, turnips, or pieces of wood
  • Neurological disorders affecting swallowing

Pathogenesis:

  • Foreign body lodges in the esophagus, most commonly at anatomical constrictions
  • Causes mechanical obstruction
  • May lead to pressure necrosis of esophageal wall

Lesions:

  • Accumulation of feed material proximal to the obstruction
  • Dilatation of the esophagus above the blockage
  • In severe cases, esophageal rupture

Clinical Signs:

  • Regurgitation of feed and saliva
  • Distress and difficulty swallowing
  • Excessive salivation
Parasitic Esophagitis

Causes: Primarily Habronema spp. and Draschia spp. (spirurid nematodes).

Pathogenesis:

  • Adult worms live in the stomach but migrate to the esophagus
  • Eggs passed in feces develop into larvae in fly intermediate hosts
  • Flies deposit larvae on lips or nostrils
  • Larvae migrate to esophagus and cause granulomatous inflammation

Lesions:

  • Granulomatous nodules in the esophageal wall
  • Ulceration and scarring
  • Potential for esophageal stricture formation
Esophagitis

Definition: Inflammation of the esophageal mucosa.

Causes:

  • Chemical irritants (acid reflux from stomach)
  • Thermal injury (hot feed or liquids)
  • Infectious agents
  • Mechanical trauma

Types:

  • Acute esophagitis: Sudden onset with severe inflammation
  • Chronic esophagitis: Persistent inflammation leading to fibrosis and stricture

Lesions:

  • Hyperemia and edema of mucosa
  • Erosions or ulcers
  • In severe cases, perforation

Forestomach Pathology

In ruminants, the stomach is divided into four compartments: rumen, reticulum, omasum, and abomasum. The first three compartments (rumen, reticulum, omasum) are collectively called the forestomachs. These compartments are lined with stratified squamous epithelium and do not contain gastric glands.

Rumen Pathology

Tympany (Bloat)

Definition: Excessive accumulation of gas in the rumen and reticulum.

Types:

  • Free-gas bloat: Gas can be released by eructation
  • Frothy bloat: Gas is trapped in foam and cannot be released

Causes:

  • Sudden change to lush legume pastures
  • Feeding large amounts of easily fermentable carbohydrates
  • Abnormal fermentation patterns
  • Mechanical obstruction of the esophagus

Pathogenesis:

  • Excessive gas production or impaired eructation
  • Compression of lungs and cardiovascular system
  • Can lead to death from respiratory or cardiac failure

Lesions:

  • Marked distension of the rumen and reticulum
  • Compression of thoracic and abdominal organs
  • Congestion and hemorrhage in lungs and other organs
Rumen Atony (Hypotonia)

Definition: Decreased or absent motility of the rumen.

Causes:

  • Sudden change in diet
  • Feeding poor quality roughage
  • Systemic diseases
  • Metabolic disorders
  • Stress or adverse environmental conditions

Pathogenesis:

  • Disruption of normal microbial fermentation
  • Accumulation of indigestible material
  • Secondary complications like acidosis

Lesions:

  • Flaccid rumen wall
  • Accumulation of fibrous material
  • Reduced or absent papillary projections
Rumenitis

Definition: Inflammation of the rumen wall.

Causes:

  • Ingestion of irritant substances
  • Severe acidosis
  • Foreign bodies
  • Secondary to systemic infections

Types:

  • Acute rumenitis: Severe inflammation with potential for systemic effects
  • Chronic rumenitis: Persistent inflammation leading to fibrosis

Lesions:

  • Hyperemia and edema of the rumen wall
  • Erosions or ulcers
  • In severe cases, perforation

Reticulum Pathology

Traumatic Reticuloperitonitis

Definition: Inflammation of the reticulum and peritoneum caused by penetration of a foreign body.

Causes:

  • Ingestion of sharp foreign objects (wire, nails, glass)
  • Most common in cattle grazing on cultivated pastures

Pathogenesis:

  • Foreign body settles in the reticular groove
  • Penetrates the reticular wall
  • May perforate the diaphragm and pericardium
  • Causes localized or generalized peritonitis

Lesions:

  • Fibrinonecrotic inflammation of the reticulum
  • Adhesions between reticulum and other organs
  • Purulent exudate in the reticular lumen
  • Potential for pericarditis if diaphragm is penetrated

Clinical Signs:

  • Reluctance to move
  • Pain on movement
  • Reduced appetite
  • Decreased milk production
Reticulitis

Definition: Inflammation of the reticular mucosa.

Causes:

  • Chemical irritants
  • Severe acidosis
  • Secondary to systemic infections

Lesions:

  • Hyperemia and edema of the reticular mucosa
  • Erosions or ulcers
  • Potential for perforation

Omasum Pathology

Omasal Impaction (Omasal Blockage)

Definition: Accumulation of dry, coarse feed material in the omasal leaves.

Causes:

  • Feeding poor quality, coarse roughage
  • Dehydration
  • Reduced omasal motility

Pathogenesis:

  • Accumulation of indigestible material in omasal leaves
  • Reduced omasal motility
  • Secondary complications

Lesions:

  • Distended omasal leaves filled with dry feed material
  • Dehydration of omasal contents
  • Secondary abomasal stasis
Omasitis

Definition: Inflammation of the omasal mucosa.

Causes:

  • Severe acidosis
  • Ingestion of irritant substances
  • Secondary to systemic infections

Types:

  • Acute omasitis: Severe inflammation with potential for systemic effects
  • Chronic omasitis: Persistent inflammation leading to fibrosis

Lesions:

  • Hyperemia and edema of the omasal mucosa
  • Erosions or ulcers
  • In severe cases, perforation

Abomasum Pathology

The abomasum is the true stomach of ruminants, equivalent to the stomach of non-ruminants. It is lined with simple columnar epithelium and contains gastric glands that secrete hydrochloric acid and pepsinogen. Pathological conditions of the abomasum can significantly impact digestion and nutrient absorption.

Abomasitis

Definition: Inflammation of the abomasal mucosa.

Causes:

  • Feeding moldy or spoiled feed
  • Sudden changes in diet
  • Ingestion of chemical irritants
  • Secondary to systemic infections
  • Displaced abomasum with subsequent ischemia

Types:

  • Acute abomasitis: Severe inflammation with potential for systemic effects
  • Chronic abomasitis: Persistent inflammation leading to atrophy

Pathogenesis:

  • Direct irritation of mucosa by causative agents
  • Disruption of normal acid secretion
  • Secondary complications like ulceration

Lesions:

  • Hyperemia and edema of the abomasal mucosa
  • Erosions or ulcers
  • In severe cases, perforation
Abomasal Ulceration

Definition: Erosion of the abomasal mucosa extending through the muscularis mucosae.

Causes:

  • Stress (transport, parturition, disease)
  • Administration of non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
  • Severe abomasitis
  • Displaced abomasum

Pathogenesis:

  • Breakdown of mucosal defense mechanisms
  • Increased acid production or decreased mucus production
  • Ischemia in displaced abomasum cases

Lesions:

  • Single or multiple ulcers in the abomasal mucosa
  • Hemorrhage and perforation in severe cases
  • Secondary complications like peritonitis

Clinical Signs:

  • Anorexia
  • Weight loss
  • Dark feces (melena) due to bleeding
  • Colic in severe cases
Displaced Abomasum

Definition: Abnormal positioning of the abomasum from its normal anatomical location.

Types:

  • Left Displaced Abomasum (LDA): Abomasum moves between the left abdominal wall and the rumen
  • Right Displaced Abomasum (RDA): Abomasum moves to the right side of the abdomen
  • Abomasal Volvulus: Twisting of the displaced abomasum

Causes:

  • Primary atony of the abomasum
  • Secondary to other gastrointestinal disorders
  • Periparturient period in dairy cattle

Pathogenesis:

  • Accumulation of gas and fluid in atonic abomasum
  • Buoyancy causes displacement
  • In volvulus cases, vascular compromise leads to necrosis

Lesions:

  • Distended abomasum filled with fluid and gas
  • Ischemia and necrosis in volvulus cases
  • Secondary complications like ketosis

Clinical Signs:

  • Reduced appetite
  • Decreased milk production
  • Ketotic breath and milk
  • Characteristic splashing sound on auscultation

Stomach Pathology

In non-ruminants, the stomach is a single-chambered organ lined with simple columnar epithelium containing gastric glands. The stomach serves as a reservoir for food and initiates protein digestion through acid and enzyme secretion.

Gastritis

Acute Gastritis

Definition: Acute inflammation of the gastric mucosa.

Causes:

  • Ingestion of irritant substances (chemicals, plants, foreign bodies)
  • Infectious agents (bacteria, viruses)
  • Drugs (NSAIDs)
  • Stress
  • Systemic diseases

Pathogenesis:

  • Direct irritation or infection of gastric mucosa
  • Disruption of mucosal barrier
  • Inflammatory response with edema and hyperemia

Lesions:

  • Hyperemia and edema of gastric mucosa
  • Erosions or ulcers
  • Mucoid or hemorrhagic exudate

Clinical Signs:

  • Anorexia
  • Vomiting (in species capable)
  • Abdominal pain
  • Dehydration
Chronic Gastritis

Definition: Persistent inflammation of the gastric mucosa.

Causes:

  • Persistent irritants
  • Autoimmune mechanisms
  • Chronic infections
  • Helicobacter spp. infection

Pathogenesis:

  • Ongoing inflammatory response
  • Progressive mucosal damage
  • Atrophy of gastric glands
  • Intestinal metaplasia in severe cases

Lesions:

  • Thickened gastric mucosa
  • Loss of normal rugal pattern
  • Atrophy of gastric glands
  • Lymphoplasmacytic infiltration

Clinical Signs:

  • Gradual onset of signs
  • Weight loss
  • Intermittent vomiting
  • Anemia in severe cases

Gastric Ulceration

Gastric Ulcers

Definition: Erosion of the gastric mucosa extending through the muscularis mucosae.

Causes:

  • Stress (transport, disease, parturition)
  • NSAIDs administration
  • Severe gastritis
  • Increased acid production

Pathogenesis:

  • Breakdown of mucosal defense mechanisms
  • Increased acid exposure
  • Necrosis of mucosal cells
  • Progression to deeper tissue layers

Lesions:

  • Round or oval defects in gastric mucosa
  • Hemorrhage at ulcer margins
  • Fibrous repair in chronic cases
  • Perforation in severe cases

Clinical Signs:

  • Colic (horses)
  • Anorexia
  • Weight loss
  • Dark feces (melena) if bleeding occurs
